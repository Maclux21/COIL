The Mărțișor is held on the 1st of March. As a sign of spring, men and women alike gift to the most important women in their life a white-and-red ribbon, tied to any sort of charm (whether that consists of a pin, a bracelet, or any sort of jewelry). The tradition involves wearing the small object for the first part of March. Later, on the 9th of the month, the ribbon is tied to a tree to help it blossom. The white string signifies purity, as well as a new beginning, while the red one symbolizes life, energy and vitality.
People dress in warm, traditional clothing and gather while holding candles, symbolizing light, faith, and hope during the cold winter season. These gatherings are often connected to religious celebrations, where communities come together to pray, sing, or take part in rituals that have been passed down for generations. The soft glow of the candles creates a peaceful and reflective atmosphere, reinforcing a sense of unity and spiritual connection. Beyond their religious meaning, these moments highlight how important togetherness, tradition, and shared values are in Romanian culture, especially during significant seasonal celebrations.
Babele (in translation, “old women”). The tradition lasts 8 days, from March 1st to the 8th of the month. Each person is assigned a day, based on their date of birth. To find out your date, you must add up the digits of your birthday and divide that number by 2. Traditionally, the weather of the day you are assigned predicts how the rest of the year will go for you. Although it is quite superstitious, many people find themselves calculating the exact date of their babă, hoping it will be a sunny and peaceful one.
Dragobete (coming from the Romanian translation of the word “love” - dragoste). What the rest of the world celebrates on Valentine’s day, Romania celebrates ten days after. It honours love, youth and courtship, and it has rooots in mythology. People find it deeply meaningful, as it unites the society among one single purpose: to show love. Nowadays, Romanians celebrate love twice within 10 days.
Urători are traditional Romanian New Year customs in which groups of people, often children or young men, go from house to house reciting poems, singing, and making rhythmic sounds with bells or whips. These performances are meant to wish health, prosperity, and good luck for the coming year. The tradition is lively and symbolic, often including costumes and sometimes masks, and it plays an important role in bringing communities together while preserving cultural heritage.
Romanians pass traditions down through a mix of family practices, school activities, and community events. In families, grandparents and parents teach children directly by involving them in traditions—like cooking traditional dishes for Easter and Christmas, going caroling, or taking part in New Year customs such as urători. In schools and kindergartens, teachers organize cultural days where students learn folk dances, wear traditional clothing, and create handmade items like mărțișoare. Communities also play a role through local festivals, church events, and village celebrations, where children can see and participate in authentic customs. By repeatedly experiencing these traditions in everyday life, children grow up understanding both how to practice them and why they matter.
Childhood is closely tied to participation in traditions—children go caroling during Christmas, take part in Eurători on New Year’s, and celebrate events like Mărțișor by making and giving small handmade gifts. These moments are often shared within the extended family, reinforcing strong bonds and a sense of belonging. Traditions are not only practiced but also celebrated with pride, making them a visible and meaningful part of daily life and personal identity from a young age.
